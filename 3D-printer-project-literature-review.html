3D Printing Technologies

  1. Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM)

  • also known as Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF)

  • melting and extruding plastic filaments through a nozzle

  • inexpensive and easy to use

  • ideal for hobbyists and small businesses

  1. SteroLithography (SLA)

  • uses a laser to solidify a liquid photopolymer resin

  • high accuracy

  • ideal for small, detailed parts

  1. Digital Light Processing (DLP)

  • similar to SLA, but uses a projector to project a 2D image onto a liquid photopolymer resin

  • high accuracy

  • high speed (faster than SLA)

  1. Selective Laser Sintering (SLS)

  • uses a laser to fuse powdered materials

  • strong and durable parts

  • ideal for ideal for manufacturing and functional prototyping

  1. Selective Laser Melting (SLM)
  • similar to SLS but instead of fusing heterogenous materials together it is typically used with a mono material such as pure aluminum or titanium.
  1. DMLS - Direct Metal Laser Sintering

  • is similar to SLS in principal

  • Typically alloys such as Cobolt Steel.

  • The only difference from a SLS machine is the technology used.

  • The bed chamber of such a system must be heated to and maintained at around 100 degrees of the materials melting point and a powerful Lazer like a CO2 Lazer is required to fused the metal powder together.

  • SpaceX uses this technology to build the engine for its falcon rockets.

  1. Binder Jetting

  • uses a liquid binder to fuse powdered materials

  • used for large, complex parts

  • ideal for architectural models and industrial applications

  1. Electronic Beam Melting (EBM)

  • Powerful electron beams fuse metal powders

  • Used to make intricately detailed metal parts.

  1. Laminated Object Manufacturing (LOM)
  • Layers of paper, metal or plastic are glued or welded and cut to shape.
  1. Material Jetting

  • Inkjet-like print heads deposit droplets of material layer-by-layer.

  • Achieves intricate details.

  1. Directed Energy Deposition

  • Focused thermal energy fuses materials as they are deposited.

  • Used to repair and coat metal parts.

  • examples:

  • Laser Engineered Net Shaping: LENS systems use a high power laser (400W to 3kW) to fuse powdered metals into fully dense three-dimensional structures. Here the powder is sprayed from nozzle and a laser simultaneously fuses the deposited in to required shape layer by layer. LENS is owned by Optomec.

  • Electron Beam Additive Manufacturing: It is similar to LENS but uses Electron Beam instead of a Laser. The technology is owned by Sciaky company. The machines are significantly bigger and Parts and structures up to 19 ft. x 4 ft. x 4 ft. (5.79 m x 1.22 m x 1.22 m) or round parts up to 8 ft. (2.44 m) in diameter can be produced with Sciaky’s EBAM machines.

Major 3D printer parts

  1. Frame

  • Linear Rod

  • cheaper (10x to 100x)

  • Types

  • metal (mostly aluminum)

  • carbon fiber

  • very light

  • requires processing due to friction

  • enforces an offset between the frame and the extrusion head due to clamps

  • ridden by linear bearings

  • Linear Rail

  • only one degree of freedom (preferred)

  • long spans can be easily supported withouth obstructing the traversing path

  • Types:

  • T-Rails

  • V-Rails (preferred)

  • ridden by carriages

  • a plate with chamfered wheels attached on them

  • can be calibrated for some inaccuracies caused by custom construction at home

  • through flanged accentric bolts used to attach the wheels

  • center of linear rail is usually untapped (unthreaded)

  1. Stepper Motors

  • dominant standard NEMA (National Electrical Motor Association)

  • NEMA XX, where the size of the face plate is X.X" by X.X"

  • doesn’t specify the height

  • doesn’t specify the gearing

  • operate in open loop (doesn’t need close loop - encoders - as typical DC motor for precise rotation)

  • longer motors have more torque, but larger ones (with a higher NEMA number) have even more torque

  • usually NEMA 17 is used, NEMA 23 is an overkill in a hobbyist size 3D printer

  • very low power efficiency, consume current even if there’s no motion

  • recommend torque for hobbyist 3D printer is 50 to 60 N.cm

  • gearing for stepper motors are usually use planary gearing

  • types:

  1. Permanent Magnet

  • rotors that are constructed with permanent magnets

  • higher torque

  1. Variable Reluctance

  • rotor constructed from iron, with gear like shape

  • lower torque

  • higher accuracy

  1. Hybrid Syncronous

  • has permenant magnet core

  • circumference is built from plain metal in a gear like shape

  • high torque

  • high resolution

  • completes full rotation in 200 full steps

  • or in 400 half steps (microstepping)

  • types

  • bi-polar (most common)

  • uni-polar

  2. Extruders

  • nozzel size is usually specified in microns (1/1000 mm)

  • nozzle materials:

  • brass (cheapest)

  • copper

  • hardened steel

  • componenets of extruder

  • ceramic heater

  • nozzle

  • hotend

  • thermistor

  • usually actively cooled to avoid jamming due to heat creep

  • extrusion drive

  • extrusion gears:

  • hobbed, have contours

  • drive, typical gear, pulls with teeth

  • extrusion drive placement:

  • direct

  • printing process is less responsive (specifically the reverse driving - retraction)

  • indirect

  • print head (extruder) can acclerate/decalerate faster

  • usually accepts 1.75mm filaments

  1. Printing Bed
  • should have heating to avoid wrapping caused by rapid cooling after deposition

  1. Linear Carriages

  2. Transimissions

  • belt-driven

  • pully and belt (traditional)

  • belt and pinion (very rarely used due to inerital changes during traversal)

  • lead-screw-driven

  • reduce backlash (affects accuracy)

  1. Motherboard & Firmwares

  • it is preferable to have an open-source motherboard, with open-source firmware

  • it is also perferable to have a widespread motherboard and firmware to ease the troubleshooting

  • finally it is preferable to have a minimal firmware with limited set of features to be able to grasp and analyze the firmware

  • niche features may be strapped on later without the need to understand their internals (such as GUI)

  • Marlin Firmware is a great choice, with wide array of microcontrollers supported

  • major supported microcontorller/OS:

  1. AVR Atmega (used in Arduino)

  2. ESP32 (a microcontroller with built-in wifi and bluetooth connectivity)

  3. native linux

  4. STM32 (high performace microcontroller series usually used for quadcopters)

  • Reprep Firmware is a another choice, with limited set of microncontroller supported, but with additional niche features (such as a web interface)
  1. Power Supply

  • typical voltage range is 12v or 24v

  • higher voltage is preferable

  • stepper motors will have higher performace

  • lighter wires can be used as they’ll be carrying less current

  • this is least component we can cheap on, bad manufacturing can be disasterous

  1. Logistics

  • Calibration

  • this is the longest step in “assembling a hobbyist 3D printer”

  • a 3D printer is not only calibrated in general, but also calibrated for a given print model, required properties, printing material and other factors (maybe weather)

  • includes calibrating the PID controller of the heating element in the extruder

  • if the printed calibration cube have any slant, then the axis are probably slanted

  • Slicing

  • Microstepping

  • full step size is 1.8 degrees, the half step size is 0.9 degrees

  • to obtain smooth approxmiated curves it is recommended to have an axis resolution of 50 microns (0.05 mm)

  • large step sizes cause the motors to overshoot or “ring” produceing noises and inaccurcies

  • in a nutshell, it is when a motor overshots it designated postion, then returns to it

  • microstepping reduce the step size to 0.1125 degrees

  • reduces the motor noise greatly

  • a step is usally divided into 16 microsteps

  • higher divisions exist but with diminishing returns

  • microstepping capable drivers are expensive

  • their microcontrollers should be fast enought to calculate the divisions

  • additionally their electronics should be able to gradually switch current from one phase to another, and not just switch them
