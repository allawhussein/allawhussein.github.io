3D Printing Technologies

  1. Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM)
    • melting and extruding plastic filaments through a nozzle
    • inexpensive and easy to use
    • ideal for hobbyists and small businesses
  2. SteroLithography (SLA)
    • uses a laser to solidify a liquid photopolymer resin
    • high accuracy
    • ideal for small, detailed parts
  3. Digital Light Processing (DLP)
    • similar to SLA, but uses a projector to project a 2D image onto a liquid photopolymer resin
    • high accuracy
    • high speed (faster than SLA)
  4. Selective Laser Sintering (SLS)
    • uses a laser to fuse powdered materials
    • strong and durable parts
    • ideal for ideal for manufacturing and functional prototyping
  5. Selective Lazer Melting (SLM)
    • similar to SLS but instead of fusing heterogenous materials together it is typically used with a mono material such as pure aluminum or titanium.
  6. DMLS - Direct Metal Laser Sintering
    • is similar to SLS in principal
    • Typically alloys such as Cobolt Steel.
    • The only difference from a SLS machine is the technology used.
    • The bed chamber of such a system must be heated to and maintained at around 100 degrees of the materials melting point and a powerful Lazer like a CO2 Lazer is required to fused the metal powder together.
    • SpaceX uses this technology to build the engine for its falcon rockets.
  7. Binder Jetting
    • uses a liquid binder to fuse powdered materials
    • used for large, complex parts
    • ideal for architectural models and industrial applications
  8. Electronic Beam Melting (EBM)
    • Powerful electron beams fuse metal powders
    • Used to make intricately detailed metal parts.
  9. Laminated Object Manufacturing (LOM)
    • Layers of paper, metal or plastic are glued or welded and cut to shape.
  10. Material Jetting
    • Inkjet-like print heads deposit droplets of material layer-by-layer.
    • Achieves intricate details.
  11. Directed Energy Deposition
    • Focused thermal energy fuses materials as they are deposited.
    • Used to repair and coat metal parts.
    • examples:
      • Laser Engineered Net Shaping: LENS systems use a high power laser (400W to 3kW) to fuse powdered metals into fully dense three-dimensional structures. Here the powder is sprayed from nozzle and a laser simultaneously fuses the deposited in to required shape layer by layer. LENS is owned by Optomec.
      • Electron Beam Additive Manufacturing: It is similar to LENS but uses Electron Beam instead of a Laser. The technology is owned by Sciaky company.
